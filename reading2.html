Reading Response #2

Nesrine Malik – AI Slop

Nesrine Malik’s article about “AI slop” made me realize how much artificial content has quietly mixed into my daily life. I didn’t even have a term for it before reading this, but now I see it everywhere. She describes two parallel image channels: one is real images of the world, and the other is AI-generated content that looks real but isn’t. That idea really stood out to me. It feels like we are constantly switching between reality and something synthetic without even noticing. Sometimes I don’t even stop to question what I’m looking at.

Something I found especially disturbing was her point about how AI content spreads through platforms like WhatsApp, where there are no public comments to question it. The content feels more trustworthy because it comes from someone you know personally. That made me think about older relatives or people who may not fully understand how advanced AI has become and how realistic it can look. If something appears real and also confirms what you already believe, it becomes very easy to accept it as truth. The issue is not just misinformation, but how convincing and emotionally targeted it can be. It doesn’t just inform people; it reinforces their existing beliefs in subtle ways.

Malik also discusses how AI often reinforces nostalgic and conservative imagery. The examples of idealized white families or “trad wife” content show that AI isn’t neutral. It pulls from existing data that already contains bias and repeats those patterns. That made me realize that technology isn’t just a neutral tool. It reflects cultural norms and sometimes even exaggerates them. It’s unsettling to think that something automated and profit-driven can shape political and social imagination so powerfully.

Another idea that really affected me was her explanation of desensitization. When everything from serious political events to silly AI fantasy images appears in the same endless scroll, it all starts to feel flattened. Real suffering, war, or injustice becomes just another piece of content between memes and aesthetic videos. I’ve definitely experienced this before — seeing something shocking online but then immediately scrolling past it because the algorithm keeps feeding me more. The speed and volume of visual media make it difficult to process what is actually happening in the real world. Everything feels urgent, but nothing feels real enough to act on.

As a media studies student, this article feels especially relevant. We are learning how to create images and digital content, but Malik reminds us that visual culture has consequences. AI slop isn’t just harmless entertainment. It changes how we understand reality. It creates confusion, emotional overload, and sometimes even influences politics. What scares me most is her idea that we are “sleepwalking into disaster,” not because we lack information, but because we are overwhelmed by distorted versions of it. This reading made me want to be more intentional about what I consume and what I create. If the internet is becoming flooded with artificial content, then being thoughtful and transparent in media production feels more important than ever. Reality already feels unstable, and adding endless synthetic layers on top of it only makes it harder to stay grounded.
