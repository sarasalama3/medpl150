“Motherhood in Crisis” was really emotional and intense to read. Instead of just giving statistics or facts about maternal healthcare in Sierra Leone, the graphic novel format made the experiences feel much more personal and real. Each chapter focused on different women dealing with pregnancy, childbirth, medical complications, social pressure, and loss. One of the main ideas throughout the stories was how dangerous motherhood can become when women don’t have proper healthcare or support systems. The stories also showed how women are often blamed for reproductive issues because of cultural expectations surrounding motherhood and fertility.

What stood out to me the most was how the visuals created emotion. The illustrations used lighting, shadows, facial expressions, and color to show fear, stress, sadness, and isolation. A lot of the scenes were visually dark, especially during moments involving labor, illness, or death. Some panels used warm orange lighting from candles or dim rooms, which made the scenes feel emotional and intimate at the same time. Other scenes used rain, darkness, or empty space to emphasize loneliness and grief. I also noticed that the pacing of the panels slowed down during emotional moments, which forced the reader to really focus on the characters’ facial expressions and body language.

I think the graphic novel format made the information more powerful than reading a normal article would have. Seeing illustrations of the women in hospitals, homes, villages, and recovery spaces made it easier to emotionally connect with what they were going through. The visuals made the fear and uncertainty surrounding childbirth feel very real. I also thought it was interesting how the story combined illustrations with real interviews and documentary-style information because it blurred the line between journalism and art. The stories were educational, but the visuals made them much more memorable.

This connects to our media class because it shows how visual communication and design can affect the way audiences understand serious issues. The artists used composition, color, framing, pacing, and typography to guide the reader’s emotions and attention throughout the story. The graphic novel shows that visual storytelling can be used not only for entertainment, but also to spread awareness about important social and humanitarian issues. The visuals communicate emotion in a way that feels more immediate and personal than statistics alone.
